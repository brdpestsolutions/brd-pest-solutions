After Your Service

Why am I still seeing pests after my first treatment?

This is the most common question we get — and it almost always has the same answer: it's normal, and it means the treatment is working.

Our products eliminate pests at the source, not just the ones you can see on the surface. That process takes 7–10 days. As pests try to escape treated areas, you may actually see more activity for a few days. They're not moving in — they're moving out.

Give it 10 days. If you're still seeing significant activity after that, call us. We'll come back at no charge — that's what the guarantee is for.

How long does it take for pest control to fully work?

Full results typically take 2–3 treatment cycles. The first visit eliminates active pests; subsequent visits build the exterior barrier that prevents re-entry and breaks the full pest lifecycle.

This is why one-time treatments are less effective than recurring plans — pests breed continuously, and one treatment can't catch every life stage.

About Pest Problems

Does my home have to be dirty to get pests?

No. Clean homes get pests too — and you shouldn't feel ashamed if you do.

Pests are looking for three things: food, water, and shelter. Even a spotless home offers all three. You can wipe down counters, sweep floors, and store food in sealed containers — and still see ants marching across your kitchen like they own the place.

Entry points matter more than cleanliness. Gaps in weatherstripping, cracks in the foundation, and spaces around pipes are how most pests get in — regardless of how clean the interior is.

When are pests most active?

It depends on the pest. Mosquitoes and fire ants peak in summer. Termites swarm in late winter and spring. Rodents move indoors in fall as outdoor food sources dry up and temperatures drop. Cockroaches and spiders are year-round problems in most climates.

Year-round coverage beats reactive treatment for exactly this reason — different pests become problems at different times, and a recurring plan keeps you protected through all of them.

DIY vs. Professional Treatment

Do DIY pest control methods actually work?

For minor, isolated pest activity, DIY products can reduce numbers temporarily. But they almost never solve the underlying problem — because they target the pests you can see, not the colony, the entry point, or the full lifecycle.

Professional treatment uses products and application methods not available retail, and targets the source: colonies, nesting areas, breeding sites, and entry points. The difference isn't just stronger chemicals — it's a fundamentally different approach.

Wasting time on methods that don't work means pests keep thriving while you think you're solving the problem.

Do ultrasonic pest repellers work?

No. Multiple scientific studies show ultrasonic devices have no meaningful effect on pests — they don't repel rodents, insects, or spiders. The research is consistent and clear.

Save the $30–50 and spend it on weatherstripping and caulk to seal actual entry points. That will do more for your pest situation than any electronic device on the market.

Termites

Does homeowner's insurance cover termite damage?

Typically no. Most homeowner's insurance policies exclude termite damage because it's considered a preventable problem — not a sudden, accidental event like a fire or storm.

The numbers make preventive treatment an easy decision: termites cause over $5 billion in property damage annually in the US, the average repair bill is $3,000, and severe infestations can exceed $10,000. An annual inspection costs a fraction of that.

By the time most homeowners realize there's a problem, they've been living with it for months — because termites eat from the inside out, and damage isn't visible until it's advanced.

What are the early signs of termites?

The most common early signs include:

Mud tubes — pencil-width tunnels along your foundation walls, often tan or brown in color. Subterranean termites use these to travel between soil and wood.

Swarmer wings — discarded wings near windows and doors in late winter or spring, left behind when reproductive termites emerge to start new colonies.

Hollow-sounding wood — tap wooden beams, floors, or window frames. A hollow sound suggests termites have been feeding inside.

Small holes in drywall — sometimes accompanied by small piles of pellets (drywood termite droppings).

If you spot any of these, don't wait. The longer termites go undetected, the more expensive the damage.
