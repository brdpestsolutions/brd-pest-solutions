5 Signs You Have a Termite Problem (And What to Do About It)

May 8, 2025 6 min read By Marcus Webb

Termites cause more than $5 billion in property damage every year in the United States — more than fires, floods, and storms combined. The frustrating part? Most homeowners don't realize they have a problem until the damage is already extensive. Termites work from the inside out, hollowing out structural wood while leaving the painted surface intact.

The good news is that termites do leave clues. Our technicians find these five warning signs on nearly every inspection where an active infestation is confirmed. If you spot any of them, don't wait — the longer a colony has to establish, the more expensive remediation becomes.

Sign 1: Mud Tubes on Foundation Walls

Subterranean termites — the most common species in the southeastern and central U.S. — can't survive in open air. They build pencil-width mud tubes from soil to wood, using them as protected highways to move between their underground nest and their food source.

Check the exterior foundation of your home, crawl space walls, basement walls, and the inside of your garage. Active tubes will be moist and soft. Even if tubes appear dry and abandoned, have them inspected — termites often abandon surface tubes temporarily while expanding deeper into the structure.

Sign 2: Hollow-Sounding or Damaged Wood

Tap suspected areas with the handle of a screwdriver. Solid wood produces a dense thud. Termite-damaged wood sounds hollow and papery — because the insects consume the interior grain while leaving a thin outer shell intact. In severe cases, a screwdriver pressed gently against the surface will puncture straight through.

Common locations: door frames, window sills, baseboards, floor joists in crawl spaces, and the wood framing around any plumbing penetrations.

Sign 3: Frass (Drywood Termite Droppings)

Drywood termites — common in coastal and southern states — push their fecal pellets out of small holes in the wood they're infesting. This material, called frass, looks like tiny wood-colored pellets or fine sawdust. You'll typically find small piles of it directly below kick-out holes in infested wood.

Don't confuse frass with ordinary sawdust. Termite frass has a uniform, hexagonal pellet shape when viewed closely and doesn't crumble into powder between your fingers.

"Termites work silently for months before the signs become obvious. By the time homeowners notice, we're usually looking at damage that took 12 to 18 months to accumulate."

Sign 4: Discarded Wings Near Doors and Windows

Once a termite colony reaches maturity (typically after 3–5 years), it produces winged reproductives called alates or "swarmers." These insects emerge in swarms — usually in spring, often after rain — to mate and establish new colonies. After mating, swarmers shed their wings, leaving small piles of translucent, equal-length wings near light sources, windowsills, or doorways.

A termite swarmer looks similar to a winged ant, but with key differences: termites have straight antennae, a thick waist with no pinching, and two pairs of equal-length wings. If you find piles of wings and can't identify whether they're from termites or ants, save a sample in a plastic bag for your technician to inspect.

Sign 5: Tight-Fitting Doors or Warped Windows

As termites damage the wood in door frames and window frames, the structural changes can cause warping that makes doors and windows difficult to open or close. This is especially noticeable in summer months, when humidity alone is insufficient to explain the problem.

Any sudden change in how doors or windows operate — without a corresponding shift in humidity or season — is worth investigating, particularly if your home is older or sits in a high-termite-pressure area like Texas, Georgia, or the Southeast generally.

What to Do If You Find These Signs

Don't attempt to treat termites with consumer products. Retail sprays and store-bought bait stations are designed for perimeter prevention — they're not capable of reaching an established colony buried in your structure or soil.

  • Don't disturb the area. Breaking open mud tubes or poking into damage before an inspection can cause the colony to relocate deeper into the structure, making treatment harder.
  • Schedule a professional inspection immediately. A licensed technician can determine species, assess extent of damage, and recommend the appropriate treatment protocol.
  • Document what you find. Photos of mud tubes, frass piles, and damaged wood help your technician identify the type and likely entry point of the infestation before they arrive.
  • Ask about a warranty. Reputable pest control companies offer renewable termite warranties that cover re-treatment and, in some cases, repair costs if termites return after treatment.

The most important thing you can do right now is act quickly. Termite colonies grow over time, and so does the cost of remediation. An inspection costs you nothing — the damage from waiting can cost thousands.
